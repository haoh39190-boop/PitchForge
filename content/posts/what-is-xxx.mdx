Customer follow-up is more than contacting someone repeatedly. A useful conversation usually starts by answering three questions.

## What stage is the customer in?

A new lead needs a natural opening and need confirmation. A customer who has already spoken with you wants to know that you remember the last discussion. During negotiation, the conversation should return to vehicle fit, trim differences, ownership cost, and the overall plan.

An inaccurate stage creates two common problems: pushing before the customer is ready, or sending language so generic that there is no reason to continue.

## What does the customer actually care about?

“Is there enough space?” and “Can the price move?” are surface questions. Practical context is more useful:

- Who drives the vehicle and how many people usually ride in it?
- How much of the use is commuting, long-distance driving, or family travel?
- Is the budget a hard ceiling or an overall range after trade-in and financing?
- Which other models are being compared, and on what dimensions?

The more the follow-up reflects real context, the less it feels like a bulk message.

## Is the next step concrete enough?

A strong ending does not simply ask, “Have you decided?” It proposes an easy next action:

- Would later today or tomorrow work for a short conversation?
- During a visit, should we start with rear-seat space or the trim plan?
- Would a comparison of the relevant trims within the same budget help?

PitchForge organizes stage, priorities, concerns, and channel into structured follow-up language. The sales advisor should still refine the result with what was learned in the real conversation. Customers need to feel understood, not covered by a polished script.
